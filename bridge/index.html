Complex Numbers, bridge example

Complex numbers appear often in science and engineering. The suspension of a car, the electronics in a phone, skyscrapers and bridges all use complex numbers in their design.

However, the most important (and interesting) examples are advanced—so, when you start learning about complex numbers it can appear that they have little practical use. This is far from true.

For example, differential equations are perhaps the most powerful tool in mathematics; and complex numbers are essential to their application. But, differential equations are not easy. However, if we take a simple approach—focusing on what we can do, rather than what we can't— we can appreciate the importance of complex numbers in the real-world.

Extending Eulers equation

Say we are designing a car's suspension, of phone's electronics—some dynamic system that changes with time ($t$). Chances are, the system is desribed by a differential equation with a solution $$ y=e^{st} $$ Here $s=a+ib$ is a complex number that we have to (somehow) find. We can use Euler's equation to understand what $e^{st}$ looks like $$ \begin{eqnarray} y &=& e^{st} \nonumber \\ &=& e^{(a+ib)t} \nonumber \\ &=& e^{at}e^{ibt} \nonumber \\ &=& e^{at} \left( \cos bt + i\sin bt \right) \nonumber \end{eqnarray} $$

Illustrate graphically. Sliders for a and b perhaps. Show how real solution is found?

Eigenstuff

How do we find the right complex number $s$? Lets consider our first (and the simplest possible) differential equation: $$ \frac{dy}{dt}=0 $$ This equation just says that $y$ doesn't change with time. Substituting $y=e^{st}$, we have $$ se^{st} = 0 $$ This equation is solved by $s=0$, so a solution is $$ y = e^{st} = e^{0t} = 1 $$ And, that's our recipe: substitute $y=e^{st}$ and solve for $s$.

Bridge

Much more interesting problems can be solved with the same recipe—including the design of bridges. Check out this video. In engineering, design choices (materials, structures, etc.) determine the differential equation. In the case of this bridge, the equation that describes the angle of the twist ($y$) is $$ \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + 2\zeta\frac{dy}{dt} + y=0 $$ Here $\zeta$ (zeta) is a designed parameter—engineers selected it through their design choices. We can solve this equation with the same recipe as above; substituing $y=e^{st}$ we have $$ \begin{eqnarray} s^2\cancel{e^{st}}+2\zeta s \cancel{e^{st}} + \cancel{e^{st}} &=& 0 \nonumber \\ s^2+2\zeta s + 1 &=& 0 \nonumber \end{eqnarray} $$ We can solve for $s$ by completing the square: $$ \begin{eqnarray} (s+\zeta)^2-\zeta^2+1&=&0 \nonumber \\ (s+\zeta)^2 &=& \pm \sqrt{\zeta^2-1} \nonumber \\ s &=& -\zeta \pm \sqrt{\zeta^2-1} \nonumber \end{eqnarray} $$ Now, with hardly any further math, we can see what happened to the bridge. Say $\zeta$ is less than zero and greater than -1 ($-1<\zeta<0$). Then, the value of the square-root above is the imaginary part of $s$ (i.e., $b$) and the real part of $s$ is $-\zeta$ (i.e., $a$), which is positive.

Recalling that $ y = e^{st} = e^{at}e^{ibt}$—what happens to $e^{at}$ as $t$ gets large (i.e., as time progresses)? What would a better choice of $\zeta$ have been?

s-domain

Many engineers hardly ever consider differential equations directly (if at all). Instead, complex numbers allow them to focus just on the critical values of $s$—since these determine the behaviour of their system. Technically, these special complex numbers are known as the "poles" and "zeros"—something to look forward to at engineering school.

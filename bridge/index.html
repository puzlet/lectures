Complex Numbers, bridge example

Martin Clark and Gary Ballantyne
May 2016

[This web page is currently under development.]

Complex numbers appear often in science and engineering. The suspension of a car, the electronics in a phone, skyscrapers and bridges all use complex numbers in their design.

However, the most important (and interesting) examples are advanced—so, when you start learning about complex numbers it can appear that they have little practical use. This is far from true.

For example, complex numbers have a pivotal role in differential equations—perhaps the most powerful tool in mathematics. But, differential equations are not easy, and, for most, their first serious encounter will be at university. However, if we take a simple approach—focusing on what we can do, rather than what we can't— we can see why complex numbers are so important in the real-world.

Extending Eulers equation

Say we are designing a car's suspension, or phone's electronics—some dynamic system that changes with time ($t$). That system is very likely described by a differential equation: that is, an equation involving differentials of some quantity (say $y$). Just for example:

$$ \frac{dy}{dt}+y=0 $$

Let's first think carefully about what this equation says. There is a function of time, $y=y(t)$; we don't know what it is; we only know that the sum of the function and its first derivative is zero.

Now, more often than not, a solution to the differential equations you'll encounter (including the one above) is

$$ y=e^{st} $$

where $s=\sigma+i\omega$ is a complex number that we have to (somehow) find. For example, say $s=-1+i0$, then

$$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{dy}{dt}+y &=& se^{st}+e^{st} \nonumber \\ &=& -1e^{-t}+e^{t} \nonumber \\ &=& 0 \end{eqnarray} $$

In other words, $y(t) = e^{-t}$ is a solution to our problem. To understand what $e^{st}$ looks like, consider

$$ \begin{eqnarray} y &=& e^{st} \nonumber \\ &=& e^{(\sigma+i\omega)t} \nonumber \\ &=& e^{\sigma t}e^{i\omega t} \nonumber \\ \end{eqnarray} $$

We know how $e^{i\omega t}$ behaves from an earlier lecture. Recall that $e^{i\theta}$ is a point in the complex plane at angle $\theta$ and modulus of one— so $e^{i\omega t}$ is a point whose angle ($\omega t$) changes as time $t$ increases. In other words, $e^{i\omega t}$ draws a circle in the complex plane.

On the other hand, $e^{at}$ is just a regular exponential function. For positive values of $a$, as $t$ increases, $e^{at}$ will get bigger, and for negative values, $e^{at}$ will get smaller.

When we multiply $e^{i\omega t}$ and $e^{i\sigma t}$ together to get $e^{st}$, we combine both behaviours—the first term gives us a rotation in the complex plane, and the second term changes the modulus.

Eigenstuff

How did we know that the right value of $s$ above was $s=-1$? All we had to do was substitute $y=e^{st}$ into the differential equation and solve for $s$:

$$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{dy}{dt} + y = 0 \nonumber \\ s e^{st} + e^{st} = 0 \nonumber \\ s \cancel{e^{st}} + \cancel{e^{st}} = 0 \nonumber \\ s + 1 = 0 \nonumber \end{eqnarray} $$

Obviously, then, the solution is $s=-1$. And, so our recipe is: substitute $y=e^{st}$ and solve for $s$.

Bridge

Important problems, such as Newton's law of cooling are close cousins of the differential equation above. But, we need to go a little further—be just a little more ambitious— to find a problem that needs a genuinely complex value for $s$.

Check out this video. The equation that describes the angle of the twist ($y$) of the bridge is

$$ \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + 2\zeta\frac{dy}{dt} + y=0 $$

where $\zeta$ (zeta) is a designed parameter—engineers selected it through their design choices. We can solve this equation with the same recipe as above; substituting $y=e^{st}$ we have

$$ \begin{eqnarray} s^2\cancel{e^{st}}+2\zeta s \cancel{e^{st}} + \cancel{e^{st}} &=& 0 \nonumber \\ s^2+2\zeta s + 1 &=& 0 \nonumber \end{eqnarray} $$

Now solve for $s$ by completing the square:

$$ \begin{eqnarray} (s+\zeta)^2-\zeta^2+1&=&0 \nonumber \\ (s+\zeta)^2 &=& \pm \sqrt{\zeta^2-1} \nonumber \\ s &=& -\zeta \pm \sqrt{\zeta^2-1} \nonumber \end{eqnarray} $$

Now, lets say $\zeta$ is less than zero and greater than -1 ($-1<\zeta<0$). Then, the value of the square-root above is the imaginary part of $s$, and its real part is

$$ \sigma = -\zeta \nonumber \\ $$

Can you see what happened to the bridge? Note that $\sigma>0$, because $\zeta<0$—that is, $\sigma$ is positive. Recalling that $ y = e^{st} = e^{\sigma t}e^{i\omega t}$—what happens to $e^{\sigma t}$ as $t$ gets large (i.e., as time progresses)?

If you were the engineer that designed the bridge, what value of $\zeta$ would you choose?

s-domain

Many engineers hardly ever consider differential equations directly (if at all). Instead, complex numbers allow them to focus just on the critical values of $s$—since these determine the behaviour of their system. Technically, these special complex numbers are known as the "poles" and "zeros"—something to look forward to at engineering school.

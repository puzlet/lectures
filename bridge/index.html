Complex Numbers, bridge example

Martin Clark and Gary Ballantyne
May 2016

[This web page is currently under development.]

Complex numbers appear often in science and engineering. The suspension of a car, the electronics in a phone, skyscrapers and bridges all use complex numbers in their design.

However, the most important (and interesting) examples are advanced—so, when you start learning about complex numbers it can appear that they have little practical use. This is far from true.

Differential equations are just about the most powerful mathematical tool available. By themselves, they are too advanced for us today. But, with complex numbers we can transform an interesting (and useful) differential equation into a (much easier) quadratic equation.

Extending Eulers equation

Say we are designing some dynamic system—car suspension, or electronics or whatever—something that changes with time ($t$). Chances are, a solution to that equation is $$ y=e^{st} $$ Here $s=a+ib$ is a complex number that we have to somehow find. We can use Euler's equation to understand what $e^{st}$ looks like $$ \begin{eqnarray} y &=& e^{st} \\ &=& e^{a+ib} \\ &=& e^{a}e^{ib} \\ &=& e^{a} \left( \cos b + i\sin b \right) \end{eqnarray} $$

Eigenstuff

The question is, how do we find the right complex number $s$? Lets consider our first (and the simplest) differential equation: $$ \frac{dy}{dt}=0 $$ Substituting $y=e^{st}$, we have $$ se^{st} = 0 $$ You can easily see that this equation is solved by $s=0$, so a solution is $$ y = e^{st} = e^{0t} = 1 $$ And, thats pretty much our recipe: substitute $y=e^{st}$ and solve for $s$.

Bridge

Much more interesting problems can be solved with the same recipe— including the design of bridges. Check out this video. In engineering, through design choices (materials, structures, etc.) sets the differential equation. In the case of this bridge, the equation that describes the angle of the twist ($\phi$) is $$ \frac{d^2\phi}{dt^2} + 2\zeta\frac{d\phi}{dt} + \phi=0 $$ Here $\zeta$ (zeta) is a designed parameter—engineers chose it through their design choices. We can solve this equation with the same recipe as above; substituing $y=e^{st}$ we have $$ \begin{eqnarray} s^2\cancel{e^{st}}+2\zeta s \cancel{e^{st}} + \cancel{e^{st}} &=& 0 \\ s^2+2\zeta s + 1 &=& 0 \end{eqnarray} $$ The solutuions for s are then $$ s = -\zeta \pm \sqrt{\zeta^2-1} $$ Now, with hardly any further maths, we can see what happened to the bridge. Say $\zeta$ is less than zero and greater than -1 ($-1<\zeta<0$). Then the square-root value is imaginary part of $s$ (which we called $b$ above) and the real part of $s$ is $\zeta$ ($a$ above), which is positive. $$ y = e^{st} = e^{at}e^{ibt} $$

s-domain

Engineers who often design dyanmic systems very often don't even consider the differential equation directly—instead they understand the behaviour via the values of $s$. These special complex numbers are known as the "poles" and "zeros" of a system—something to look forward to at engineering school.

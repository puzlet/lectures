Complex Numbers, bridge example

Martin Clark and Gary Ballantyne
May 2016

[This web page is currently under development.]

Complex numbers often appear in engineering; the suspension of a car, the electronics in a phone, skyscrapers and bridges all use complex numbers in their design. However, the most important (and interesting) examples are advanced—so, when you start learning about complex numbers it can appear that they have little practical use.

Here we show the role complex numbers play in the self-destruction of a bridge (i.e., a failure of design). Although it is an advanced example, we only assume you know about differentiation. Some details we will hurry past, and other important aspects we will not even glance at. Nevertheless, it will give you a taste of the place of complex numbers in actual engineering.

Differentiation is about change, and systems that change with time are known as dynamic systems (for example, a car's suspension when it hits a bump). Dynamic systems are described by differential equations—that is, an equation involving differentials of some quantity (say $y$). Just for example, consider

$$ \frac{dy}{dt}+y=0 $$

It's worth pausing to think just what this equation says. There is a function of time, $y=y(t)$; we don't know what it is (but we'd like to); we only know that the sum of the function and its first derivative is zero. Most often we get equations like this from the underlying physics (or chemistry, or whatever)—we get clues, but not the solution.

Typically a solution to the differential equations you'll encounter (including the one above) is

$$ y=e^{st} $$

where $s=\sigma+i\omega$ is a complex number that we have to (somehow) find. For example, say $s=-1+i0$. (OK, this is not truly complex ... but hang in there.) Then, substituting into the differential equations gives

$$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{dy}{dt}+y &=& se^{st}+e^{st} \nonumber \\ &=& -1e^{-t}+e^{t} \nonumber \\ &=& 0 \end{eqnarray} $$

In other words, $y=e^{st}$, with $s=-1$ is $y=e^{-t}$, which is a solution to the differential equation above.

Before we consider a differential equation that requires a genuinely complex $s$, let's first consider what $y=e^{st}$ looks like. Substituting $s=\sigma+i\omega$:

$$ \begin{eqnarray} y &=& e^{st} \nonumber \\ &=& e^{(\sigma+i\omega)t} \nonumber \\ &=& e^{\sigma t + i\omega t} \nonumber \\ &=& e^{\sigma t}e^{i\omega t} \nonumber \end{eqnarray} $$

So, $y=e^{st}$ is the product of two exponentials—one real, and one complex. We know how the complex exponential ($e^{i\omega t}$) behaves from an earlier lecture . Recall that $e^{i\theta}$ is a point in the complex plane at angle $\theta$ and modulus (distance from the origin) of one—so $e^{i\omega t}$ is a point whose angle ($\omega t$) changes as time $t$ increases. In other words, $e^{i\omega t}$ draws a circle in the complex plane.

On the other hand, $e^{\sigma t}$ is just a regular exponential function. For positive values of $\sigma$, as $t$ increases, $e^{\sigma t}$ will get bigger, and for negative values of $\sigma$, $e^{\sigma t}$ will get smaller.

When we multiply $e^{\sigma t}$ and $e^{i\omega t}$ to get $e^{st}$, we combine both behaviours—the first term changes the modulus, and the second term causes a rotation in the complex plane.

How did we know that the right value of $s$ above was $s=-1$? Just substitute $y=e^{st}$ into the differential equation and solve for $s$:

$$ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{dy}{dt} + y = 0 \nonumber \\ s e^{st} + e^{st} = 0 \nonumber \\ s \cancel{e^{st}} + \cancel{e^{st}} = 0 \nonumber \\ s + 1 = 0 \nonumber \end{eqnarray} $$

Obviously, then, the solution is $s=-1$. And, our recipe to solve differential equations is: substitute $y=e^{st}$ and solve for $s$.

Important problems, such as Newton's law of cooling are close cousins of the differential equation above. But, we need to go a little further—be just a little more ambitious—to understand what happened to the bridge.

Say $y$ is the angle of the bridge's (dynamic) twisting. Physics tells us something about about $y$:

$$ \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + 2\zeta\frac{dy}{dt} + y=0 $$

Here $\zeta$ (zeta) is a designed parameter—engineers selected it through their choice of the bridges materials, structures and their assumptions about the environment (including the wide). Let's say $\zeta$ is greater than -1 but less than 1 ($-1<\zeta<1$). We can solve this equation with the same recipe as above; substituting $y=e^{st}$ we have

$$ \begin{eqnarray} s^2\cancel{e^{st}}+2\zeta s \cancel{e^{st}} + \cancel{e^{st}} &=& 0 \nonumber \\ s^2+2\zeta s + 1 &=& 0 \nonumber \end{eqnarray} $$

The equation above is a quadratic equation—we seek the two values of $s$ for which the left-hand side (LHS) equals zero. It's worthwhile considering what the LHS looks like for real values of $s$:

Note that (in the range of $\zeta$ we're considering), for real $s$, the LHS is never zero. So, to get a solution, we need to look for complex $s$. Solve for $s$ by completing the square:

$$ \begin{eqnarray} s^2+2\zeta s + 1 &=& 0 \nonumber \\ s^2 + 2\zeta s +(\zeta^2 - \zeta^2) + 1 &=& 0 \nonumber \\ (s^2 + 2\zeta s + \zeta^2) - \zeta^2 + 1 &=& 0 \nonumber \\ (s+\zeta)^2-\zeta^2+1&=&0 \nonumber \\ (s+\zeta)^2&=&\zeta^2-1 \nonumber \\ s+\zeta &=& \pm \sqrt{\zeta^2-1} \nonumber \\ s &=& -\zeta \pm \sqrt{\zeta^2-1} \nonumber \end{eqnarray} $$

Here, the value inside the square-root above is negative (since $-1<\zeta<1$), and so the square-root itself is the imaginary part of $s$. The real part of $s$ is the bit that isn't in the square root:

$$ \sigma = -\zeta $$

So, if $\zeta$ is negative ($\zeta<0$), then $\sigma$ is positive ($\sigma>0$). Recalling that $y=e^{st}=e^{\sigma t}e^{i\omega t}$—can you see what happened to the bridge? If you were the engineer that designed the bridge, what value of $\zeta$ would you choose?

Many engineers hardly ever consider differential equations directly (if at all). Instead, complex numbers allow them to focus just on the critical values of $s$—since these determine the behaviour of their system. Technically, these special complex numbers are known as the "poles" and "zeros" (something to look forward to at engineering school).

Finally, let's consider what $y=y(t)$ really looks like. The quadratic equation gave us two values for $s$ (from the $\pm$ of the square root)—call them $s_1$ and $s_2$. By themselves, $e^{s_1t}$ and $e^{s_2}t$ are complex—but the twist of the bridge is real. The trick is to use both values for $s$— $y=e^{s_1t}+e^{s_2t}$.
